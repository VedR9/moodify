# User Interview Syntheses — Part 2
## Spotify Music Discovery Research

> **Methodology note:** These 5 profiles are synthesized composites derived from 13 survey responses (collected June 16–26, 2026). Each profile represents a distinct behavioral archetype identified through pattern analysis. Quotes are reconstructed from open-ended survey responses (Q18–Q20) and reflect real language used by respondents.

---

## User 1 — "The Mood Seeker"
**Profile:** Priya, 24 | Software engineer | Listens multiple times a day | Spotify Free → switches to YouTube Music when frustrated

**Survey signal:** Importance 5/5 · Stuck: Frequently · Repeat ratio: 5/10 · Context ease: 2/5 · Explain useful: 4/5

### In her own words:
> *"I search for 'peaceful music for focused work' and I keep getting the same lo-fi playlists. It's not wrong, but it's also not right. I want something calm and instrumental but not generic. The app doesn't understand that 'productive and peaceful' is a specific feeling — it just hears 'calm' and gives me the same 10 tracks everyone else gets."*

> *"I don't want Spotify to know my genre. I want it to know my mood. Those are completely different things."*

### Core pain:
The algorithm understands **genre history**, not **emotional intent**. When she's looking for something specific — late-night focus music, post-run wind-down, melancholic but not sad — the search results are either too broad (lo-fi) or too familiar (same artists she already knows).

### Workaround:
Asks friends on WhatsApp. Checks Instagram Reels when she hears a song she likes. Uses YouTube search with very specific descriptions ("late night Japanese city pop rainy window").

### What she'd want from AI:
> *"I'd tell it: I'm working late, it's been a stressful day, I need something that keeps me focused but doesn't feel clinical. Give me music I've never heard that matches this exact feeling — and tell me why you picked it."*

### Key insight for product:
She's not looking for music recommendation. She's looking for **emotional translation** — turning a feeling into a playlist. The gap isn't the catalogue, it's the interface.

## User 2 — "The Stuck Loyalist"
**Profile:** Rahul, 28 | Marketing professional | Listens multiple times a day | Spotify Premium

**Survey signal:** Importance 3/5 · Stuck: Frequently · Repeat ratio: 8/10 · Context ease: 2/5 · Explain useful: 3/5

### In his own words:
> *"I know I'm listening to the same songs. I know it. But after a 10-hour workday, opening Spotify and hitting shuffle on my saved playlist takes zero effort. Searching for something new takes effort. And most times when I do try, the recommendations are just... more of what I already have."*

> *"I'd love better discovery but I'm not going to work for it. If it doesn't happen automatically, it's not happening."*

### Core pain:
**Cognitive fatigue is the gatekeeper.** He cares about discovery in principle but not enough to invest effort after a long day. The current app asks him to browse, search, and curate — all of which feel like work. He needs discovery to come to him, not the other way around.

### Workaround:
None. He defaults to his "Liked Songs" playlist on shuffle and mentally accepts that he's stuck.

### What he'd want from AI:
> *"Just play me something I've never heard before that I'll actually like. Don't ask me anything. Don't show me a screen. Just do it. Prove to me once that you know me, and I'll trust you."*

### Key insight for product:
The **first successful unsolicited discovery moment** is everything for this user. He won't initiate — but if the product surprises him once with something perfect, he'll open the door. Zero-friction, proactive discovery is the only thing that breaks his loop.

## User 3 — "The Active Hunter Hitting Walls"
**Profile:** Arjun, 22 | Music enthusiast / college student | Listens daily | Spotify Free

**Survey signal:** Importance 5/5 · Stuck: Rarely · Repeat ratio: 3/10 · Context ease: 2/5 · Explain useful: 5/5

### In his own words:
> *"I use Discover Weekly every single week. I actually like the concept. But it used to find me things I'd never heard of — now it's basically a slightly remixed version of my existing library. The algorithm has learned me too well and stopped taking risks."*

> *"I want it to take me somewhere genuinely unfamiliar. Not a different song by an artist I already follow. An artist I've never encountered, in a style adjacent to what I love but not identical to it."*

### Core pain:
He's the most engaged user — and the most disappointed. **The algorithm has calcified around his taste profile.** Discover Weekly, which was once his favourite feature, now feels like a mirror rather than a window. He wants to cross genre boundaries and find underground artists, but every surface the platform offers keeps pulling him back to what it already knows he likes.

### Workaround:
Reddit (r/ifyoulikeblank, r/indieheads), YouTube rabbit holes, Bandcamp new releases, asking niche music Discord communities.

### What he'd want from AI:
> *"Build me a playlist of lesser-known artists across contemporary classical, jazz, and world music that matches my current mood, actively avoids songs I've overplayed, and introduces genuinely unique discoveries — not just popular songs from those genres."*

### Key insight for product:
**The over-personalisation trap.** The algorithm optimises for completion and satisfaction, which means it converges on safe bets over time. For power users like Arjun, this is a betrayal of the product's original promise. An "exploration mode" that deliberately deprioritises his listening history could unlock this.

## User 4 — "The Sequence Planner"
**Profile:** Vikram, 35 | Working professional | Listens a few times a week | Switches between Spotify and YouTube Music

**Survey signal:** Importance 4/5 · Stuck: Frequently · Repeat ratio: 10/10 · Context ease: 3/5 · Explain useful: 4/5

### In his own words:
> *"Music isn't just about the song. It's about the journey. I want something that starts with driving energy, moves into something more reflective, then ends with something soft and familiar. No app does this. They give you playlists that are all one mood."*

> *"My entire week is basically familiar tracks on repeat. Not because I want it that way — because creating the right sequence for my mood takes too long, and I don't have that time."*

### Core pain:
He thinks in **arcs, not tracks**. He wants music that evolves with him across an evening, a commute, a workout — not a flat mood board. The platform's playlist model is too static for how he actually experiences music. His 10/10 repeat ratio isn't laziness — it's the cost of a product that can't do what he needs.

### Workaround:
Maintains multiple manually curated playlists for different moments. Spends time on Sunday building playlists for the week. Still ends up on familiar tracks when he runs out of time.

### What he'd want from AI:
> *"I want to say: start with something that feels like a car ride at night, move through something charged and energetic, then bring me down to something emotional and quiet by the end. Like a DJ who knows my taste setting a vibe for the evening."*

### Key insight for product:
He's describing **dynamic playlist sequencing** — a fundamentally different product paradigm from static playlists or single-song recommendations. This is a genuine white space that AI makes possible for the first time.

## User 5 — "The Context-Blind Faithful"
**Profile:** Meera, 32 | Homemaker / occasional listener | Listens a few times a week | YouTube Music Premium

**Survey signal:** Importance 3/5 · Stuck: Frequently · Repeat ratio: 8/10 · Context ease: 2/5 · Explain useful: 5/5

### In her own words:
> *"I was looking for old Doordarshan music — the kind that plays in the background of a quiet Sunday morning. I couldn't find it. The search doesn't understand what I mean by that. I get Bollywood from the 90s, or classical, but not that specific feel."*

> *"I just want simple categories. Old, new, retro, singer-based, mood-based. Make it easy for someone like me who doesn't know the name of every genre to find what they're looking for."*

### Core pain:
**The vocabulary gap.** She knows exactly what she wants to feel but doesn't have the music-industry language to describe it. Search requires knowing genre names, artist names, era labels — all of which are barriers for a casual but emotionally specific listener. The platform speaks music industry; she speaks feelings.

### Workaround:
Asks family members. Relies on YouTube's autoplay sidebar. Searches YouTube with descriptive phrases ("old Hindi morning songs soft").

### What she'd want from AI:
> *"If I could tell it: find me songs for a quiet Sunday morning, mix of old Hindi and international, something that feels nostalgic and calm — and it actually understood that — that would be everything."*

### Key insight for product:
This user represents a massive underserved segment — emotionally literate but **music-vocabulary-poor**. Natural language discovery isn't just for power users. For casual listeners who know what they *feel* but not what they *want to search for*, it's the only interface that works.

## Cross-User Patterns

| Pattern | Users | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Mood ≠ Genre (apps conflate them) | 1, 2, 4, 5 | Core product gap — emotional intent is invisible to the algorithm |
| First successful surprise = trust unlock | 2, 3 | One perfect unsolicited recommendation changes behaviour |
| Cognitive effort kills discovery | 2, 4, 5 | Discovery must come to the user, not require active search |
| Over-personalisation trap | 3, 4 | Algorithm converges too fast — needs deliberate exploration mode |
| Vocabulary barrier | 5, 1 | Natural language is the only accessible interface for non-power users |

## What This Means for the MVP

Every user in this research is describing the same underlying product:
> **A music assistant that understands what you want to feel — not just what you've listened to before — and delivers it with zero browsing effort.**

The delivery mechanism differs by user (proactive for User 2, on-demand for User 1, sequential for User 4) but the core capability is identical: **emotional and contextual understanding that today's algorithm cannot provide.**
